## Purpose

Standard structures for communicating AI risk to three audiences with different needs: the AI Governance Committee (decision-makers), affected business/technical teams (operational), and — where required — external stakeholders (regulators, customers, the public).

## 1. Committee Risk Briefing (Internal, Decision-Oriented)

Use for escalating a High or Critical risk requiring Committee approval of a treatment strategy.

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| Risk summary | One paragraph — what the risk is, which system, why it matters |
| Current score | Likelihood × Impact = Score, Rating |
| Business context | What business process/decision depends on this system |
| Treatment options considered | List each option with trade-offs (cost, timeline, residual risk) |
| Recommendation | The specific action being requested for approval |
| Decision needed by | Date, and consequence of delay |
| Owner | Who will execute if approved |

## 5. General Principles for All Risk Communication

- Lead with the decision or action needed, not the full narrative — put context after, not before.
- State uncertainty honestly. A risk assessment that rounds up to "compliant" or down to "no concern" when the picture is genuinely mixed is a governance failure of its own, not a favor to the reader.
- Match the level of technical detail to the audience — a Committee briefing needs the business consequence, not the model architecture; a technical team update needs the reverse.
- Always name the next decision point and who owns it. A risk update with no clear owner or deadline tends to stall.
